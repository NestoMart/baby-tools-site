FAQ

Common Questions

Everything parents ask about due dates and pregnancy timing.

Your due date is calculated using Naegele's Rule: add 280 days (40 weeks) to the first day of your last menstrual period. If you know your conception date, we add 266 days (38 weeks) instead. Only about 5% of babies are born on their exact due date, so a window of 37–42 weeks is full term.
The first trimester spans weeks 1–12, the second trimester weeks 13–26, and the third trimester weeks 27–40. Each trimester brings distinct developmental milestones for your baby and significant physical changes for you.
Yes. An early ultrasound (before 14 weeks) can provide a more accurate due date by measuring the baby's size. If there's more than a 5–7 day difference between the LMP-based date and the ultrasound date, your provider may adjust your due date accordingly.
If you don't know your LMP, you can use your conception or ovulation date using the second tab. Alternatively, an early ultrasound is the most accurate way to establish a due date. Our calculator supports both methods.
Not exactly. 40 weeks is closer to 9 months and 1 week. Pregnancy is measured in weeks for precision. The 9-month figure is an approximation — counting from the LMP through delivery is 40 weeks or about 280 days total.
