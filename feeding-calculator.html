FAQ

Feeding Questions

Common questions about baby feeding schedules and amounts.

Newborns should be fed every 2–3 hours, or 8–12 times per day. They have tiny stomachs and breast milk or formula is digested quickly. Never let a newborn go more than 4 hours without feeding in the early weeks, as they need frequent nutrition for healthy growth.
A general rule is 2.5 oz of formula per pound of body weight per day. For example, a 10-pound baby needs about 25 oz per day, spread across 6–8 feedings (roughly 3–4 oz per feeding). Always follow your pediatrician's guidance for your specific baby's needs.
The AAP recommends starting solid foods around 6 months. Signs of readiness include sitting up with minimal support, showing interest in food, and loss of the tongue-thrust reflex. Start with single-ingredient purees and introduce one new food every 3–5 days to watch for allergies.
Signs that your baby is well-fed include 6+ wet diapers per day after the first week, regular bowel movements, steady weight gain, appearing satisfied after feedings, and alert and active behavior when awake. If you're concerned, consult your pediatrician or a lactation consultant.
Cluster feeding is when a baby feeds very frequently over a short period, often in the evening. It's common in breastfed newborns and during growth spurts. Cluster feeding helps stimulate milk supply and is completely normal — not a sign of insufficient milk production.
