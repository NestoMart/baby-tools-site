FAQ

Sleep Questions

Common questions about baby sleep schedules and wake windows.

Newborns (0–3 months) need 14–17 hours of sleep per day, including frequent naps. They sleep in short 2–4 hour cycles and wake to feed. It's normal for newborns to have no set schedule in the early weeks — their sleep-wake cycles aren't yet tied to day and night.
A wake window is the amount of time a baby can comfortably stay awake between sleep periods before becoming overtired. Wake windows grow as babies get older — from just 45–60 minutes for newborns to 4–6 hours for toddlers. Respecting wake windows helps babies fall asleep more easily.
Most babies transition from 2 naps to 1 nap between 14–18 months. Signs include resisting one of the naps, difficulty falling asleep at bedtime, or naps getting shorter. The transition is usually complete by 18 months, though some babies keep 2 naps until closer to 18 months.
Most babies do best with a bedtime between 6:30–8:00 PM. Babies have a natural melatonin surge in the early evening, making this the ideal window. An overtired baby who misses this window may fight sleep harder — known as getting a "second wind."
Signs of an overtired baby include rubbing eyes, pulling ears, yawning, staring blankly, becoming fussy or clingy, arching their back, or hyperactivity. Catching your baby at the first signs of tiredness and putting them down before they become overtired helps them fall asleep much more easily.
